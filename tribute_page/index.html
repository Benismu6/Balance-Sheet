Shirley Chisholm

"If they don't give you a seat at the table, bring a folding chair"

Here is a timeline of Shirley's life:

  • November 30, 1924: Shirley Anita St. Hill (later known as Shirley Chisholm) is born in Brooklyn, New York, to Caribbean immigrant parents. Growing up in a working-class neighborhood, she developed a strong sense of social justice and equality.
  • 1942: Graduates from Girls' High School in Brooklyn, where she excelled academically and was a member of various clubs and societies, laying the foundation for her future activism and leadership roles.
  • 1946: Earns a Bachelor of Arts from Brooklyn College, where she majored in sociology and became involved in campus politics and the Harriet Tubman Society, fostering her interest in civil rights and social change.
  • 1949: Marries Conrad Chisholm, a private investigator, and starts her career in education while continuing to be active in community organizations and political causes.
  • 1952: Receives a Master of Arts in Elementary Education from Columbia University, furthering her commitment to improving the quality of education for all children, particularly those from marginalized communities.
  • 1953: Becomes an educational consultant for the New York City Division of Day Care, advocating for affordable childcare services and better educational opportunities for minority children.
  • 1964: Elected to the New York State Assembly, representing a district in Brooklyn, where she focused on issues such as education, childcare, and unemployment, becoming known for her outspoken and fearless advocacy.
  • 1968: Elected to the U.S. House of Representatives, representing New York's 12th congressional district, where she continued her advocacy for civil rights, women's rights, and economic justice. As the first African American woman in Congress, she faced discrimination and marginalization but remained a steadfast voice for the underrepresented.
  • 1972: Announces her candidacy for the Democratic presidential nomination, challenging the status quo and advocating for progressive policies such as education reform, healthcare access, and social welfare programs.
  • 1972: Becomes the first major-party African American candidate to run for President of the United States and the first woman to run for the Democratic Party's presidential nomination, breaking barriers and inspiring a new generation of diverse political leaders.
  • 1973: Co-founds the Congressional Black Caucus, a group dedicated to addressing the legislative concerns of African Americans and promoting racial and economic justice within Congress.
  • 1983: Retires from Congress after serving seven terms, leaving a legacy of trailblazing leadership and advocacy for marginalized communities.
  • 1991: Receives the Spingarn Medal from the NAACP in recognition of her lifelong dedication to civil rights and social progress.
  • January 1, 2005: Dies in Ormond Beach, Florida, at the age of 80, leaving behind a legacy as a pioneer, a champion for equality, and an inspiration to generations of Americans.
